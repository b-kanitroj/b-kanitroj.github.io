Mapping Disparities: Infrastructure, Access, and Societal Challenges in Thailand

How do disparities in infrastructure and healthcare access impact societal challenges across Thailand's provinces?

Aim: This project investigates how disparities in infrastructure (e.g., road density) and healthcare access (e.g., number of doctors per 10,000 population) affect societal challenges like health, education, and employment issues. The goal is to identify patterns that highlight underserved provinces, offering actionable insights for policymakers. By linking these disparities to societal issues, the project seeks to provide evidence-based recommendations for improving infrastructure and welfare programs according to the needs of the people.

Chart 1: Doctor Density Across Provinces

Healthcare access varies widely across Thailand's provinces, with some areas offering significantly better doctor availability than others. The global standard of 17.2 doctors per 10,000 people, serves as a benchmark—how does Thailand measure up?

Chart 2: The Health Burden in Underserved Provinces

So what does the disparity mean for the people? This chart shows how doctor density impacts health issues and chronic disease prevalence. Provinces with fewer doctors face higher health challenges, with larger bubbles highlighting the dual strain of overall health issues and the long-term burden of chronic diseases in underserved areas.

Chart 3: Mapping Health Issues Across Provinces

This map visualizes the distribution of health issues across provinces in Thailand. The intensity of color represents the number of reported health issues in each province, allowing for easy identification of regions with higher or lower health challenges. Use the dropdown filter to explore specific health issues, or view all health issues combined to see the overall burden across the country.

Chart 4: Road Density and Societal Issues

Expanding the perspective to infrastructure, this chart explores how road density correlates with key societal issues such as employment and education challenges. Each panel compares one type of issue, with bubble sizes representing the total population in each province. The visual highlights how larger populations may either amplify or mitigate the impact of limited infrastructure. By juxtaposing these two societal dimensions, it emphasizes the compounded challenges faced by underserved regions.

Chart 5: Composite Impact of Road and Doctor Density on Societal Issues

This heatmap reveals the combined influence of road and healthcare access on societal challenges. Warmer colors indicate higher issue intensity, pinpointing regions where limited infrastructure and healthcare access converge to compound challenges. By layering these two critical dimensions, the chart underscores the interconnected nature of infrastructure disparities and their profound effects on provincial well-being.

Data Used and Access Methods: This project uses data from various sources to analyze disparities and societal challenges:

  • Infrastructure:
    • Road Density: Calculated using road distance data from the Transport Infrastructure Annual Report 2022, published by the Office of Transport and Traffic Policy and Planning, Ministry of Transport (2023), combined with provincial area data from the National Statistical Office of Thailand.
    • Healthcare Access: Measured by doctors per 10,000 population, Ministry of Public Health (2024).
  • Societal Issues: Counts of health, education, and employment issues were derived from MSO-Logbook, an individual-level dataset documenting the needs of over 900,000 people across Thailand's provinces. The raw data, available to ministry personnel upon request from the Ministry of Social Development and Human Security, required translation, parsing, and aggregation to create province-level summaries.

Although the data does not utilize APIs or fully automated pipelines, Python scripts were developed for data cleaning, aggregation, and analysis. These scripts ensure replicability with future datasets and demonstrate the potential for integrating disparate datasets. This project highlights the value of open and standardized data practices, aiming to inspire broader adoption of data-driven policymaking in Thailand. You can see the data cleaning and processing steps in my Colab notebook.

Challenges, Tools Used, and Limitations:

  • Data Cleaning: Cleaning the dataset required addressing missing values, resolving mismatched province names, and correcting inconsistent formatting. With over a million data points, significant effort went into aggregating and standardizing individual-level information into province-level summaries. Python's pandas library was instrumental in preprocessing, translating, and parsing raw datasets from Thai to ensure alignment across variables.
  • AI Assistance: Generative AI tools significantly streamlined formatting, converting, and standardizing dataset structures. While these tools provided efficiency, manual validation was essential to ensure the accuracy of data points.
  • MSO-Logbook Limitations: The data presented unique limitations. As it is collected through human-administered questionnaires, the dataset contains missing entries and inconsistencies, such as incorrectly formatted or incomplete data. Some provinces lack data entirely due to ongoing reviews, leading to charts that do not fully represent all provinces.
  • The issues uncovered during this analysis will be shared separately with the Ministry of Social Development and Human Security to encourage improvements in data collection and management practices. Addressing these gaps is vital to enhancing the utility of this rich but underutilized dataset, enabling it to better inform future policy discussions and support evidence-based decision-making within the ministry.

Conclusion: Infrastructure inequities deeply exacerbate societal challenges, leaving underserved provinces to grapple with compounded burdens on health, education, and employment.

Chart 1 establishes the baseline, revealing disparities in healthcare access across provinces. This introduces the inequity that sets the stage for further analysis. Chart 2 demonstrates how inadequate healthcare infrastructure directly impacts public health.

Chart 4 expands the perspective to road density, showing how infrastructure disparities contribute to broader societal challenges, such as education and employment issues. The patterns emphasize that infrastructure inequities are not isolated but have effects on people’s well-being. Chart 5 reinforces this interconnectedness, highlighting how the combined inadequacies of road and healthcare infrastructure compound societal challenges, with the highest burdens seen in underserved provinces.

Policy Recommendations:

  1. Targeted Resource Allocation: Direct healthcare and infrastructure investments to provinces with the most severe disparities to maximize impact where it's needed most.
  2. Integrated Planning: Address the interconnected nature of infrastructure and well-being by coordinating improvements in healthcare access and road systems to tackle compounded challenges.
  3. Evaluate Welfare Policies: Current flat-rate monthly welfare may not account for regional disparities in infrastructure. Future research should explore how these inequities affect living costs and spending to inform more equitable welfare strategies.
